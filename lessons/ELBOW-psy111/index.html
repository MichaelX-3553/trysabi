What is Psychology?

Not mind-reading. Not common sense. Something way more interesting.

🤔

Quick question. Your friend says: "I'm basically a psychologist, I can read people." Is that actually psychology? Before we define anything, think about what YOU think psychology is. Hold that thought.

Let's start with what psychology is NOT. Psychology is not mind-reading. It's not palm-reading. It's not guessing what someone is thinking based on their body language. And it's definitely not common sense, even though sometimes it confirms what people already believe.

So what IS it?

Psychology is the scientific study of behaviour and mental processes. That's the core definition. Notice two key words: "scientific" (it uses research methods, data, and evidence, not guesses) and "behaviour AND mental processes" (it covers both what you DO and what goes on inside your mind).

And here's something a lot of people miss: psychology doesn't just study humans. Psychologists like B.F. Skinner did experiments on rats, pigeons, and other animals, and then applied those findings to human behaviour too. Dennis Coon (2000) defined psychology as the scientific study of both human and animal behaviour.

The word itself gives you a clue. "Psychology" comes from two Greek words: psyche (meaning "mind") and logos (meaning "study" or "knowledge"). So literally, it's the "study of the mind."

A Quick History

People have been interested in human behaviour for thousands of years, but psychology as its own discipline only started about 145 years ago. In 1879, a German scientist named Wilhelm Wundt set up the very first psychology laboratory in Leipzig, Germany. That moment is considered the birth of modern psychology.

Then in 1890, an American named William James published the first recognized psychology textbook. After that, universities across Europe and the US started opening their own psychology labs. The field was born.

Why So Many Definitions?

If you pick up 10 psychology textbooks, you might find 10 slightly different definitions. Why? Three reasons:

  1. Psychology is a relatively new discipline compared to physics or chemistry
  2. Its theories and methods are still developing
  3. It covers such a diverse range of subjects that psychologists working in different areas see it differently
🏀

Basketball Analogy

You know how NBA scouts don't just say "that kid's nice"? They break it down: points per game, shooting percentage, assist-to-turnover ratio. They MEASURE performance. That's exactly what psychologists do with behaviour. They don't rely on vibes. They use science to describe, explain, predict, and control what people do.

The 4 Goals of Psychology (Zimbardo, 1992)

Zimbardo captured what most psychologists agree the field is trying to do in four goals. Think of these like a scouting report for human behaviour:

1

Describe

Objectively describe what people do. What happened? What behaviour are we looking at?

2

Explain

Understand WHY that behaviour happened. What caused it? What are the consequences?

3

Predict

Predict when, how, and in what form a behaviour will happen in a given situation.

4

Control

Show how it's possible to start, stop, or change a behaviour in a predictable way.

  • Psychology = the scientific study of behaviour and mental processes (humans AND animals)
  • The word comes from psyche (mind) + logos (study/knowledge)
  • Born in 1879 (Wundt's lab) and 1890 (James's textbook)
  • The 4 goals: Describe, Explain, Predict, Control

So now you know WHAT psychology studies. But how do psychologists actually THINK about the mind? Turns out, they don't all agree. There are four major ways to look at the same behaviour, and each one sees something completely different.

Chapter 02 of 16

Four Ways to Think About Minds

Same behaviour. Four completely different explanations.

🤔

Imagine a student keeps failing exams even though they study. A biologist might blame their brain chemistry. A Freudian might blame their childhood. A behaviourist might blame their study habits. A cognitive psychologist might blame their thinking patterns. Who's right? Maybe all of them.

Psychology has four major approaches, which are basically four different lenses for looking at the same behaviour. Each one focuses on a different part of the picture. None of them is "wrong," they just see different things.

1. The Biological Approach

Psychologists using this approach look for the causes of behaviour inside the body, specifically in the brain, the nervous system, and biochemical processes.

Their core idea: everything you think, feel, say, and do is caused by electrochemical events happening in your neurons. Your behaviour might also be influenced by your genes (what you inherited from your parents) and by evolution (traits that helped our ancestors survive got passed down).

If you're anxious, a biological psychologist would check your brain chemistry. If you're aggressive, they'd look at your hormone levels.

2. The Psychodynamic Approach

This one belongs to Sigmund Freud. Psychodynamic psychologists believe that most of our behaviour comes from unconscious internal conflicts, things happening in our minds that we're not even aware of.

Freud said our behaviour comes from inherited instincts, biological drives, and the tension between what we personally want and what society expects from us. He also believed that your personality is largely shaped in your first five years of life.

We'll dive deep into Freud's specific ideas in the next two chapters.

3. The Behaviourist Approach

Behaviourists like Ivan Pavlov and B.F. Skinner said: forget the mind. We can't see it, we can't measure it, so why are we studying it? Let's focus on what we CAN observe: behaviour.

This approach studies how behaviour is learned through rewards and punishments. It focuses on the environmental forces that make behaviours happen more or less often. If you do something and get rewarded, you'll do it again. If you get punished, you'll stop. That's the core idea.

4. The Cognitive Approach

Cognitive psychologists are interested in what happens between the stimulus (what you see/hear) and the response (what you do). They study thinking, information processing, perception, memory, problem-solving, and consciousness.

Unlike behaviourists, cognitive psychologists see humans as active thinkers, not passive organisms shaped only by rewards and punishments. You don't just react to the world. You think about it, rationalize, plan, and solve problems.

🏀

The film analyst says "the player misread the defence, their decision-making process was off" (Cognitive). Same play, four different explanations. Each one is valid from its angle.

Freud's Three Voices

The angel, the devil, and the referee inside your mind.

Sigmund Freud was an Austrian medical doctor who became one of the most influential (and controversial) thinkers in the history of psychology. He believed that most of what drives our behaviour is hidden beneath the surface of our awareness.

In 1923, Freud proposed that the human mind, what he called the psyche, is made up of three invisible forces constantly fighting each other. These aren't physical parts of the brain. They're hypothetical conceptualizations, Freud's way of describing how the mind works.

🤔

You're walking past a store. You see the latest Jordans. They cost ₦150,000. You have ₦200,000 saved for school fees. One voice says "BUY THEM NOW." Another voice says "That money is for school. Your parents would be disappointed." A third voice tries to find a middle ground. What's happening in your mind right now? Freud knew.

The Id: "I Want It NOW"

The Id is the most primitive part of your personality. It's completely unconscious, meaning you don't even know it's there. The Id operates on the pleasure principle: it wants instant gratification. It doesn't care about rules, consequences, or other people's feelings. It just wants what it wants, right now.

Freud said the Id contains two types of biological instincts:

  • Eros (the life instinct): drives survival activities like breathing, eating, and sex. The energy created by Eros is called libido.
  • Thanatos (the death instinct): a set of destructive forces present in all humans. When directed outward, it shows up as aggression and violence. Freud believed Eros is stronger than Thanatos, which is why people survive rather than self-destruct.

The Ego: "Let's Be Smart About This"

The Ego develops from the Id during infancy. It operates in both the conscious and unconscious mind, and follows the reality principle. Its job? Satisfy the Id's desires, but in a way that's safe and socially acceptable.

The Ego is basically your decision-maker, the part of you that says "I hear what you want, but let me figure out a realistic way to get it without getting us in trouble."

When there's conflict between the Id and Superego, the Ego steps in as the referee. It can also use defence mechanisms to protect itself from being overwhelmed by anxiety.

The Superego: "Is This Right?"

The Superego develops during early childhood (around ages 3-6) when the child starts identifying with the same-sex parent. It operates on the morality principle. This is your conscience, your moral compass, the voice that says "you should feel guilty for even thinking about that."

The Superego motivates us to behave in socially responsible and morally acceptable ways. It can also make you feel guilty if you break the rules, even rules nobody else knows about.

The Eternal Struggle

Here's the thing: these three parts of your psyche are always in conflict. The Id wants pleasure. The Superego demands morality. The Ego tries to balance both while dealing with reality. Freud said this inner conflict is the basic dilemma of all human existence.

Every decision you make, from what to eat to how to handle an insult, involves this three-way negotiation happening beneath the surface.

🏀

Basketball Analogy

The Id is that part of you that sees the rim and wants to dunk on everybody, every single play. No strategy, no game plan, just "GIVE ME THE BALL AND GET OUT OF MY WAY." Pure pleasure principle.

The Superego is the ref. It blows the whistle. "That's a charge, not a block. That's a technical foul. That's against the rules." It enforces fair play and makes you feel guilty when you break the rules.

The Ego is your basketball IQ. It says "I want to score (respecting the Id), I want to play clean (respecting the Superego), so let me find the smart play that does both." The Ego is your court vision.

Now here's where Freud gets really controversial. He said these three parts of your mind don't just appear fully formed. They DEVELOP through stages as you grow up, and if something goes wrong at any stage, it affects your personality for life. Welcome to the psychosexual stages.

Growing Up Freud

Five stages that supposedly shape your entire personality.

Freud believed children are born with a libido, a kind of pleasure energy. As they grow, this energy focuses on different body zones. If a child gets stuck at any stage, what Freud called fixation, it leaves a permanent mark on their adult personality.

🤔

Ever wondered why some adults bite their nails, chew pens, or smoke constantly? Freud would say they're stuck in a stage of development they never completed, from when they were literally babies. Wild theory? Let's see if it makes sense.

The Five Psychosexual Stages

Freud's theory says that every child goes through five stages. To be psychologically healthy, you must successfully complete each one. Think of each stage like a level in a game.. if you get stuck on a level (fixation), parts of your personality are forever affected.

Stage Age Focus Zone Key Event
Oral Birth - 1 yr Mouth Sucking, swallowing. Ego develops here.
Anal 1 - 3 yrs Anus Toilet training. Control and release.
Phallic 3 - 6 yrs Genitals Oedipus complex. Superego develops here.
Latent 6 - puberty None Sexual motivation dormant. Focus on school and social skills.
Genital Puberty+ Genitals Mature sexuality. If all prior stages completed, healthy personality.

What Happens When You Get Stuck (Fixation)

Freud said that if something goes wrong during a stage, too much pleasure or too much frustration, the person becomes fixated. This means part of their personality is permanently stuck at that stage, showing up as specific adult behaviours:

Oral Fixation

Cause: Forceful feeding, deprivation, or early weaning.

Adult behaviour: Smoking, nail-biting, pen-chewing, overeating, dependency, or verbal aggression.

Anal Fixation

Cause: Toilet training that was too harsh or too relaxed.

Adult behaviour: Too harsh = obsessive tidiness, stubbornness, meanness. Too relaxed = messy, wasteful, overly generous.

Phallic Fixation

Cause: Abnormal family setup or unusual relationship with parent.

Adult behaviour: Vanity, self-obsession, sexual anxiety, feelings of inadequacy or inferiority.

The Oedipus Complex

During the phallic stage (ages 3-6), Freud said children develop unconscious feelings toward the opposite-sex parent and see the same-sex parent as a rival. This is called the Oedipus complex (named after the Greek myth of Oedipus, who unknowingly married his mother).

Freud believed resolving this complex is how children develop their Superego. They start identifying with the same-sex parent and internalize that parent's moral standards. It's one of the most controversial parts of Freud's theory.

Important note: Most of Freud's ideas are considered controversial by modern psychologists. Many aspects lack scientific support. But his work was foundational and shaped how later psychologists thought about the mind. That's why you need to know it for your exam.

How Psychologists Investigate

Six tools psychologists use to find out what's really going on.

So psychologists want to describe, explain, predict, and control behaviour. Cool. But how do they actually DO that? You can't just guess. You need tools, specific research methods that let you collect reliable evidence. Here are the six main ones.

🤔

If you wanted to find out whether listening to music actually helps students study better, how would you set it up? Would you just ask people? Watch them? Run a test? Your answer reveals which research method fits.

1. Naturalistic Observation

This is the "watch without touching" method. The psychologist goes to where behaviour naturally happens and observes without interfering. No labs, no manipulation, just watching.

Two key rules: (a) subjects are observed in their natural environments, and (b) the observer does not attempt to interfere with the subjects' natural behaviour.

Disadvantage: What you observe might not reflect what the person truly feels inside. And the observer's own bias can affect how they interpret what they see.

2. Case Histories

A deep, detailed investigation into one specific person's background, experiences, and difficulties. Freud was probably the first to use case histories as a research method. It's mostly used in clinical settings, where a psychologist needs the full picture of a patient's life to understand their problems.

Advantage: Gives comprehensive, detailed information about one person. Limitation: Findings from one person might not apply to everyone.

3. Surveys

Questionnaires given to groups of people, either in person or through mail/online. Surveys collect data from many people at once. When the population is too large to study entirely, a random but representative sample is selected.

Advantages: Collect a lot of data quickly, low cost, easy to create and administer, cover a wide range of topics.

Disadvantages: Poor survey design can ruin the results. The answer choices might not capture what people truly feel. Response bias can skew findings.

4. Correlation Method

This method is used when you can't use an experiment. It measures whether two things are related to each other. When changes in one variable are associated with changes in another variable, there's a correlation.

Example: Psychologists want to study the relationship between divorce and juvenile delinquency. They can't force couples to divorce (that would be unethical). Instead, they observe cases of divorce and look at delinquency rates.

⚠️

Critical Point: Correlation ≠ Causation

Just because two things happen together doesn't mean one CAUSES the other. Ice cream sales and drowning rates both go up in summer. Ice cream doesn't cause drowning. Summer does. Correlation shows a relationship, NOT a cause.

5. Experimentation

This is the MOST powerful method because it's the only one that can prove cause and effect. In an experiment, the researcher deliberately changes one thing (the independent variable or IV) and measures what happens to another thing (the dependent variable or DV).

Example: To test whether a new study technique improves grades, you'd give one group the new technique (IV) and keep the other group studying normally. Then you'd compare their exam scores (DV). If the new technique group scores higher, you can say the technique caused better performance.

6. Interviews and Questionnaires

In an interview, the subject is asked questions verbally and the interviewer records responses. Interviews can be structured (fixed questions) or in-depth (flexible conversation).

A questionnaire is a written tool for gathering information. There are two formats:

  • Close-ended: Provides options to choose from (like multiple choice). Easy to analyze but limits responses.
  • Open-ended: Lets respondents write freely. Gives richer data but is harder to process and analyze.

Every questionnaire has two parts: a descriptive part (age, sex, education) and a measurement part (attitudes, opinions, beliefs).

Chapter 06 of 16

The Schools That Built Psychology

Six groups. Six visions. One field.

As psychology grew, different groups of thinkers had very different ideas about what the field should focus on. Some said "study the structure of the mind." Others said "forget the mind, study behaviour." These disagreements created schools of thought, each with a founder and a core belief.

Some of these schools died out because their methods couldn't hold up. Others are still around today, integrated into modern psychology.

🤔

If you were building psychology from scratch, what would you focus on: what the mind IS made of, what it DOES, or what people actually DO? Your answer would have put you in one of these schools.

1. Structuralism

Founded by: Wilhelm Wundt (Germany)

Structuralism tried to find the structural properties of mind and consciousness. Their method was called analytic introspection, a technique where trained people carefully examined their own conscious experiences and tried to break them down into basic elements.

They identified 3 components of consciousness: objective sensations (what you perceive), subjective feelings (how you feel about it), and mental images. They wanted to know what consciousness is made of and in what quantity.

2. Functionalism

Founded by: William James (Harvard University, USA), based on Charles Darwin's Evolutionary Theory

James argued that psychology should focus on the adaptive functions of behaviour, not the structure of consciousness. He didn't care what the mind was MADE of. He cared about what the mind DOES and why.

James saw the mind as a functional organ that helps individuals survive. He had a practical approach and wanted psychology to address relevant, everyday human concerns. He's often called the "Father of Contemporary Psychology."

3. Behaviourism

Founded by: John B. Watson and B.F. Skinner

Watson believed that structuralism, functionalism, and psychodynamic theories were basically superstitions because you can't measure what they claim to study. You can't measure "consciousness" or "unconscious desires." So why bother?

Behaviourists argued that psychology should only study observable, measurable behaviour. Watson specifically said that internal events like thoughts, images, feelings, and intentions are unmeasurable and should NOT be part of psychology's subject matter.

4. The Gestalt School

Founded by: Max Wertheimer

While structuralists were trying to break consciousness into tiny pieces, the Gestalt psychologists said: stop. People don't experience the world in pieces. They experience it as organized wholes.

Their famous motto: "The whole is greater than the sum of the parts." Think about it. A song isn't just a collection of individual notes. The WAY those notes are organized together creates something entirely new, something none of the individual notes could create alone.

Much of their work on the psychology of perception is still relevant and widely studied today.

5. Psychoanalysis

Founded by: Sigmund Freud (Vienna, Austria)

We already covered Freud's ideas in depth. As a school, psychoanalysis focused on unconscious causes of behaviour. The key concepts: the unconscious, Eros and Thanatos, Id/Ego/Superego, libido, infantile sexuality, and the idea that personality is essentially complete by age five.

Freud believed free will was an illusion and that behaviour was motivated by unconscious instincts. His method of study and therapy was called free association, where patients talked freely about themselves, and Freud interpreted what they said in terms of unconscious motives and defence mechanisms.

The major goal of psychoanalysis: bring repressed material to consciousness.

6. Humanism

Founded by: Abraham Maslow, Carl Rogers, and Rollo May (1950s)

The humanists looked at behaviourism and psychoanalysis and said: both of you are missing the best part of being human. Behaviourism treats people like trained animals. Psychoanalysis treats everyone as driven by unconscious conflicts. Where's the free will? Where's the ability to make choices?

Humanists believe that people are basically good and will naturally strive to achieve positive social goals and the highest and best for themselves. They emphasize every person's potential for self-development and personal growth.

🏀

Basketball Coaching Styles

Structuralism = the coach who films every game and breaks down every play into tiny pieces: footwork, hand position, eye movement. Functionalism = the coach who asks "does it work? Does it win games?" and doesn't care about details as long as the player is effective. Behaviourism = the coach who only looks at stats: points, rebounds, assists. Psychoanalysis = the coach who sits the player down and asks about their childhood, fears, and dreams. Humanism = the coach who says "I believe in you. You have everything you need inside you to be great."

Chapter 07 of 16

Where Psychology Works

10 fields. 10 ways psychology shows up in real life.

Psychology isn't just one thing. It's branched into many specialties, each focusing on a different slice of human experience. From the courtroom to the classroom to the hospital, psychologists are literally everywhere.

🤔

Someone has nightmares and crippling anxiety. A child is failing in school and acting out. A company wants to improve worker safety. A court needs to know if a suspect is mentally fit for trial. Different problems, different psychologists. Can you guess which specialty handles which?

1. Developmental Psychology

Studies human growth, covering physical, emotional, and intellectual development from the prenatal stage all the way to death. If you want to understand how babies learn to walk or why teenagers take risks, this is the field.

2. Educational Psychology

Uses psychology to help children in the educational system. If a child is struggling with schoolwork or being disruptive in class, an educational psychologist examines the child to figure out what's causing the problem. They study the mental, physical, social, and emotional factors that influence learning.

3. Physiological Psychology

Examines the biological bases of behaviour. These psychologists study things like the biochemical reactions behind memory and learning, and how different brain parts regulate hunger, thirst, and sleep.

4. Clinical Psychology

Concerned with the diagnosis and treatment of mental, emotional, and behavioural disorders. Clinical psychologists work in hospitals, psychiatric facilities, and clinics. They help people with conditions ranging from phobias to severe mental illness.

5. Occupational Health Psychology

Focuses on the work environment and the work-family interface. These psychologists work to improve work quality, protect workers' safety, and promote well-being on the job.

6. Cardiac Psychology

Studies the heart (the cardiac system) and its connection to emotions, especially love, and the conscious thoughts and feelings of patients with heart conditions.

7. Forensic Psychology

The branch that applies psychology to crime and the legal system. Forensic psychologists deal with psychological damages, expert testimony in court, criminal profiling, brainwashing, sexual predator laws, child custody decisions, and evaluating whether suspects are mentally fit for trial.

8. Psychology of Gender

Studies the differences between females and males and how they vary in biological, social, and mental development.

9. Social Psychology

Interested in human social behaviour: attitudes, persuasion, conformity, leadership, friendship, love, and other behaviours linked to the social environment. If you've ever wondered why people follow the crowd or how friendships form, this is the field.

10. Cultural Psychology

Studies how culture affects behaviour. Your values, eating habits, language, and obedience to laws are all influenced by the culture you grew up in. Cultural psychologists study these influences across different societies.

Physiological Psych is the center, anchored in the body and the brain. Every position matters. Every specialty serves a purpose.

Nature vs Nurture

Are you born this way, or did life make you this way?

This is one of the longest-running debates in all of psychology: is human behaviour shaped more by nature (your genes, what you're born with) or nurture (your environment, your experiences, how you were raised)?

🏀

Your height comes from your parents' genes. That's nature. But your guard skills? Your handles? Your ability to play like a guard instead of a center? That's nurture. Hours of practice, watching NBA guards, playing 3v3. So which one made you, Ayo?

Nature: What You're Born With

Heredity is the transmission of parental characteristic traits to the offspring. The carrier of heredity is the gene, a minute particle within the human sex cell.

Genes are located on materials called chromosomes. Every human body cell has 23 pairs of chromosomes. Both parents donate 23 pairs each to the child at conception.

The 23rd pair is special: it's the sex chromosome. In males, it exists as XY. In females, it exists as XX. During the preparation of sex cells (sperm and ovum), chromosome pairs separate. When the sperm and ovum fuse, the chromosomes from both parents unite, automatically transmitting characteristic traits to the child through the genes.

Twin Studies: Nature's Experiment

Psychologists who want to study the influence of genetics often look at twins. There are two types:

  • Identical (Monozygotic / MZ) twins: Result from a single fertilized egg (zygote) that splits. They are genetically identical. Always the same sex.
  • Fraternal (Dizygotic / DZ) twins: Result from two different eggs fertilized by two different sperm. They are genetically no more similar than regular siblings. Can be same or different sex.

The ideal study: examine pairs of identical twins who were separated early in life and raised in different environments. If they still end up with similar traits, that suggests genetics is playing a significant role.

Research consistently shows that MZ twins raised apart do end up more similar than DZ twins, even in different environments. This supports the role of genetics, but it's not the whole story.

Nurture: Everything That Happens to You

Environment refers to all external and internal conditions affecting your existence, growth, and welfare. According to Alhassan (1983), it's the aggregate of ALL conditions that influence the child.

Environmental influence starts before you're even born. Even in the womb, a child can develop differently if the mother smokes, takes drugs, or is ill while pregnant. Once born, the child encounters a social environment, physical environment, economic environment, and cultural environment.

Social psychology provides strong evidence that situations have powerful effects on behaviour. A person who confesses to a crime in a police station might be responding to powerful situational forces, not necessarily guilt.

The Real Answer: It's Both

Modern psychologists rarely argue pure nature OR pure nurture. The current view is that almost all behaviour results from the interaction between genetic factors and the environment.

Your genes set possibilities. Your environment determines which possibilities become reality.

Pavlov's Dog

How a dog, a bell, and some food changed psychology forever.

Learning is one of the most important things we do as human beings. We learn from the moment we're born until the day we die. But what IS learning?

Kimble (1961) defines learning as a relatively permanent change in behaviour produced through practice and/or prior experience. The key words: "change in behaviour" and "prior experience/practice."

Important things learning is NOT:

  • Not due to temporary conditions (like being tired or sick)
  • Not due to maturation alone (babies crawling → walking is growth, not learning)
  • Not from drugs (that's why athletes caught doping get banned)

Learning can be for better or worse. Sewing, dancing, and kindness are learned. But so are armed robbery, hate, and prejudice.

🥁

Why does your mouth water when you smell food even before you eat? Why do your hands reach for the drumsticks the moment your pastor says "let us worship"? You've been conditioned. You just didn't know the name for it.

The Simplest Learning: Conditioning

Conditioning is the simplest form of learning. It means acquiring specific patterns of behaviour in the presence of a well-defined stimulus. Basically, learning to connect one thing with another.

Pavlov's Famous Experiment

Ivan Pavlov was a Russian physiologist (not a psychologist!) who won a Nobel Prize. He discovered classical conditioning by accident while studying how dogs digest food.

Here's what happened:

  1. Pavlov measured how much a dog salivated when given food. The food naturally triggered salivation. This is a reflex, it happens automatically.
  2. He then started ringing a bell every time he gave the dog food. Bell + food, bell + food, bell + food.. many times.
  3. Eventually, the dog started salivating just from hearing the bell, even when no food was presented.
  4. The dog even started salivating when it saw Pavlov himself, or heard his footsteps!

The stunning discovery: when two events are repeatedly paired together, the presence of one can trigger behaviours originally associated with the other, even when that other event isn't there.

🥁

Drumming Analogy

When your pastor says "let us worship" and your hands automatically reach for the drumsticks before you even think about it.. that's a conditioned response. Your pastor's voice (CS) has been paired with actual drumming (US → UR) thousands of times over the years. Now the voice alone triggers the reaching behavior (CR). Pavlov's dog didn't "understand" the bell meant food. Its brain just wired the two together through repetition. Same thing happened to you with the drums. Nobody sat you down and said "when you hear this phrase, reach for sticks." Your brain wired it automatically. Shebi you don see am now? Na so the bell take become the food for the dog's brain.

The Key Terms

Unconditioned Stimulus (US)

The food. A natural stimulus that automatically triggers a natural response. No learning needed.

Unconditioned Response (UR)

Salivation to the food. An innate reflex response. Not learned, it just happens naturally.

Conditioned Stimulus (CS)

The bell. Originally neutral (meant nothing to the dog). Only produces a response because of learning (repeated pairing with food).

Conditioned Response (CR)

Salivation to the bell. The learned/acquired response. Similar to the UR, but now triggered by the CS instead of the US.

More Key Concepts

Trial: Each paired presentation of US and CS (each time the bell and food are given together).

Acquisition: The period when the organism is learning the CS-CR association.

Generalization

When the organism responds to stimuli that are similar to the original CS but not identical. Pavlov's dog might salivate to a buzzer or a chime, not just the bell. It's the brain going "close enough."

Generalization has survival value. "Once bitten, twice shy." A painful encounter with one insect teaches you to be careful of all similar insects.

Discrimination

The opposite of generalization. The organism learns to respond only to the specific CS and not to similar stimuli. If the buzzer is never followed by food but the bell is, the dog learns to distinguish between them.

Discrimination also has survival value. It's why children are taught to beware of strangers and to tell the difference between safe and dangerous places.

Extinction

A "reversal" of the conditioned response. If you keep presenting the CS (bell) without the US (food), the CR (salivation) gradually fades away. The association weakens because it's no longer being reinforced.

Spontaneous Recovery

After extinction, the CR can suddenly reappear after some time has passed, even without reinforcement. It's like an old habit popping back up unexpectedly.

Example: A retired criminal who still plans imaginary crimes in their head, even though there's no reward for it anymore.

Applications of Classical Conditioning

  • Language learning: words paired with actions ("tap" + tapping gesture)
  • Advertising: brands paired with positive feelings
  • Drug administration: pills paired with recovery → placebo effect
  • Phobias: a neutral object paired with a frightening experience → fear of that object
  • Prejudice and stereotypes: groups paired with negative messages → automatic negative feelings

Skinner's Box

What happens AFTER you act determines whether you act again.

Pavlov showed that we can learn through association. But his dog didn't have to DO anything to get food. The food just appeared. In real life, most learning works differently. You do something, something happens, and THAT determines whether you do it again. That's operant conditioning.

🎨

Think about learning to draw. Nobody gave you food for a good sketch. But every time a drawing came out looking fire, you felt good, and you drew more. Every time a drawing looked off, that sting made you adjust your technique. The consequences of YOUR actions shaped YOUR behaviour. That's Skinner's entire theory.

Operant vs Classical: Key Differences

Operant conditioning was developed by B.F. Skinner (also called Skinnerian conditioning). Two big differences from classical conditioning:

  1. It begins with a voluntary response (not a reflex). The organism ACTS first.
  2. The organism learns the association between its response (what it does) and the consequence (what happens after).

The Law of Effect

The foundation of operant conditioning:

  • If a behaviour is followed by a positive consequence, it will be repeated more frequently
  • If a behaviour is followed by a negative consequence, it will not be repeated as frequently

Simple idea. Massive implications.

Skinner's Famous Experiment

Skinner built a special box (the "Skinner Box") and put a starved rat inside. The box had a lever. The rat could scratch the door, sit and brood, explore, or press the lever. Any time the rat pressed the lever, food pellets dropped from the roof.

Result: the rat quickly learned to press the lever more and more often.

In another experiment, a pigeon in a box pecked at different colors. Blue? No food. Red? No food. Yellow? No food. Black? Food dropped. The pigeon learned to peck only the black.

Unlike classical conditioning, the organism must make a desired response BEFORE reward is presented. The organism's own actions determine whether reward comes.

🎨

Drawing Analogy

Your drawing journey IS operant conditioning, Ayo. When a sketch came out looking good (positive reinforcement), you drew more. When it looked off, that feeling of "nah, that's not it" (mild punishment) made you adjust your technique next time. Nobody structured this for you. The consequences of your own actions shaped your behavior. Skinner would look at your art journey and say "textbook operant conditioning." You operated on your environment (drew), got consequences (good or bad results), and your behavior changed accordingly. That's literally what "operant" means: the organism OPERATES on the environment.

Types of Reinforcement and Punishment

Positive Reinforcement

Add something pleasant after a desired behaviour. The behaviour increases.

Example: Praising a child for good behaviour. Promoting a worker for high productivity.

Negative Reinforcement

Remove something unpleasant to strengthen a desired behaviour. The behaviour increases.

Example: A child does homework to avoid scolding. A rat presses a lever to stop electric shock.

Punishment

Add something unpleasant OR remove something pleasant to weaken a behaviour. The behaviour decreases.

Example: Grounding a child for breaking rules. Taking away phone privileges for bad grades.

⚠️

THE #1 Most Confused Concept in Psychology

Negative reinforcement is NOT the same as punishment. This trips up almost everyone on exams. Here's the formula:

  • Negative reinforcement = remove something bad → behaviour increases
  • Punishment = add something bad → behaviour decreases

A parent scolds a child for not doing homework = punishment. The child does homework to avoid scolding = negative reinforcement. See the difference? One is applying the bad thing. The other is doing something to ESCAPE the bad thing.

Schedules of Reinforcement

How OFTEN you reward someone matters just as much as WHETHER you reward them. Different schedules create different patterns of behaviour.

Continuous schedule: reward every single correct response. Problem? The reward eventually loses its value. Not recommended.

The intermittent (partial) schedules are more interesting and more effective:

Fixed-Interval

Reward based on elapsed time.

Example: Monthly salary. Semester exams.

Pattern: Scalloping.. lazy after reward, then burst of effort right before the next one. Students cram before exams, workers hustle before payday. Least efficient schedule.

Fixed-Ratio

Reward after a fixed number of responses.

Example: Getting paid per item produced. Commission on sales.

Pattern: High output, but quality may suffer as people rush to get more rewards.

Variable-Interval

Reward based on varying time.

Example: Surprise quizzes. Random inspections by the boss.

Pattern: Steady effort because you never know when the reward/check is coming. Prevents scalloping.

Variable-Ratio

Reward after a varying number of responses.

Example: Slot machines. Fishing (you never know which cast catches the fish).

Pattern: Best schedule. Highest, steadiest rate of response. Most resistant to extinction.

Rules for Effective Punishment

Punishment doesn't always work. For it to be effective:

  1. It must immediately follow the offence (so the offender connects the two)
  2. It must be consistently applied (random punishment leads to guesswork)
  3. It must suit the offence (not too mild, not too severe)
  4. It should NOT be immediately followed by reward (otherwise the offender thinks the punisher feels sorry)

Practical Applications

  • Programmed Instruction: Teaching machines designed on operant principles, presenting info and requiring the learner to respond. Correct answers are reinforced immediately.
  • Behaviour Modification: Using reinforcement to shape behaviour in classrooms. Teachers pay attention to desired behaviour and ignore disruptive behaviour. The "don't" rules are always paired with "do" alternatives.
  • Token Economy: A system where desired behaviours earn tokens that can be exchanged for actual rewards.

Learning by Watching

You don't always have to touch the fire to know it burns.

Classical and operant conditioning both involve the learner directly experiencing something. But think about it: did you learn EVERYTHING through personal experience? Of course not. You watched other people. You copied what worked for them. You avoided what got them in trouble.

🏀

When you watch Thomas Agbo pull off a move on the court and then try it yourself in your next game, what kind of learning is that? You didn't get rewarded or punished. You didn't pair two stimuli together. You just watched, remembered, and copied.

Social Learning Theory

Albert Bandura (1962) proposed Social Learning Theory. His core idea: learning is a continuous interaction between human beings and the social environment. We learn by watching others, who Bandura called models.

Models can be live persons (your captain, your parents, your teacher) or symbolic models (people on TV, in movies, on social media).

Vicarious learning is learning from other people's experiences. You don't have to make the mistake yourself, you can learn from watching someone else make it. Like learning from history.

Unlike classical and operant conditioning which involve trial and error, observational learning is "no trial" learning. You learn without personally acting and getting consequences.

🏀

Thomas Agbo Analogy

Ayo, you and Thomas Agbo. That's Bandura's entire theory in real life. When you watch Thomas cook on the court, here's what's happening in your brain:

  1. Attentional process: You're locked in on his moves because he's really good and you respect his game. His skill and status as captain grabs your attention.
  2. Retention: You remember the move after the game. You replay it in your head.
  3. Motor reproduction: Next time you play, you try that move yourself. Your body attempts to copy what your brain stored.
  4. Reinforcement: If the move works and you score, you keep using it. If it doesn't work, you might try again or drop it.

Four steps. That's social learning.

Bandura's 4 Mechanisms

1

Attention

You must pay attention to the model. Physical attractiveness and high status make models more effective.

2

Retention

You must remember what you observed. If you can't retain it, you can't reproduce it later.

3

Motor Reproduction

You must be able to translate what you observed into action. Watching isn't enough, you must be able to physically do it.

4

Reinforcement

You must have motivation. If you expect rewards from imitating the model, you're more likely to reproduce the behaviour.

Important Implications

  • Children learn from what parents DO, not what they SAY. If parents don't practice what they preach, children will copy what parents DO.
  • TV violence increases aggressive behaviour in young viewers. They watch violent models and imitate.
  • Media coverage of riots can actually increase the spread of riots. Coverage provides a model for imitation.
  • Observational learning also shows how values, self-control, and practical skills are passed from person to person.

The Art of Attention

Your brain can't multitask. Here's why.

You've learned how we acquire new behaviour. But there's a gatekeeper that determines whether you learn anything at all: attention. If you're not paying attention, nothing gets in. Nothing gets stored. Nothing gets learned.

🤔

Be honest. Right now, as you're reading this on your phone in bed, how many other things is your brain doing? Are you hearing sounds? Feeling the bed beneath you? Thinking about food? The fact that you're focused on THESE WORDS instead of all that other input? That's attention at work.

What IS Attention?

William James (1890) gave one of the most famous definitions: "Attention is the taking possession of the mind, in clear and vivid form, of one out of what may seem several simultaneously possible objects or trains of thought. It implies withdrawal from some things in order to deal effectively with others."

In simpler terms: attention is how you actively select which information to focus on while filtering out everything else.

Titchener added that attention is "a state of sensory clearness with a margin and a focus." Things you perceive clearly are at the focus. Things you're dimly aware of are at the margin.

Your Brain Can't Multitask

Here's a fact that surprises most people: the brain is a sequential processor. You can do automatic things simultaneously (like talk and breathe), but when it comes to higher-level tasks, your brain processes them one at a time.

Large fractions of a second are consumed every time the brain switches between tasks. Research shows:

  • Driving while talking on a phone is like driving drunk. Phone talkers are half a second slower to hit the brakes and get in more accidents.
  • Error rates go up 50% when you try to multitask.
  • Tasks take twice as long when you're splitting attention.

Mind-Wandering

Sometimes attention shifts to matters unrelated to what you're doing. You're in a lecture and suddenly you're thinking about what to eat afterwards. That's called mind-wandering or spontaneous thought. When this happens, you lose focus on the lecture and may not understand what was said.

Five Types of Attention

Attention isn't just one thing. There are five types, arranged from simplest to most complex:

Level 1

Focused Attention

The ability to respond to specific visual, auditory, or tactile stimuli. You lock onto one thing.

Example: A goalkeeper watching only the ball during a penalty kick.

Level 2

Sustained Attention

The ability to maintain a consistent response during continuous, repetitive activity.

Example: A security guard monitoring cameras for 8 hours straight.

Level 3

Selective Attention

The capacity to maintain focus despite distracting or competing stimuli. "Freedom from distractibility."

Example: Studying while your roommate plays loud music, and actually succeeding.

Level 4

Alternating Attention

The capacity for mental flexibility that lets you shift focus between tasks with different cognitive demands.

Example: A chef moving between checking the oven, stirring the pot, and chopping vegetables.

Level 5

Divided Attention

The highest level. The ability to respond to multiple tasks simultaneously.

Example: A drummer playing a complex rhythm while singing along to the song.

Overt vs Covert Attention

Overt attention: Directing your sense organs toward a stimulus. You physically turn your head, move your eyes, point your ears toward something.

Covert attention: Mentally focusing on something without moving your sense organs. You're looking at the lecturer but mentally thinking about something else. Or you're looking straight ahead but paying attention to someone in your peripheral vision.

Interestingly, research suggests these two systems might be connected: when you covertly attend to something on your right, your eyes actually want to move in that direction, and you have to actively suppress the urge.

What Grabs Your Attention?

Two categories of factors influence attention:

Objective factors (characteristics of the stimulus itself):

  • Intensity of the stimulus (louder, brighter = more attention)
  • Repetition (repeated stimuli catch attention)
  • Size (bigger = more noticeable)
  • Movement (moving objects draw the eye)
  • Change (any change in the environment pulls attention)

Subjective factors (qualities of the observer):

  • Habit or familiarity (you notice what you know)
  • Immediate need (hungry? You notice food everywhere)
  • Profession or interest (a musician hears the drums in a song before anything else)
  • Innate tendencies (some stimuli naturally grab human attention)

Focus and Margin

Attention divides your experience into two zones:

  • Focus: Events you perceive clearly. This is where your spotlight is pointed.
  • Margin: Events you're only dimly aware of. You know they're there, but vaguely.

These zones are constantly shifting. What's at the focus one moment may move to the margin the next, and vice versa.

🏀

Mid-Game Awareness

When you're driving to the basket, the rim is your FOCUS, crystal clear, locked in. Your defender, the crowd, Thomas yelling for the ball, the scoreboard? That's the MARGIN. You're vaguely aware of all of it, but it's blurry, background noise. Then the whistle blows. Play stops. Suddenly you notice your ankle hurts and someone in the crowd is playing music. Focus and margin just switched. What WAS in the margin is now the focus, and the rim doesn't matter anymore.

Serial vs Parallel Processing

Imagine standing between two groups having two different conversations:

  • Parallel processing: Picking up bits of BOTH conversations simultaneously. Possible for simple input, but difficult to maintain.
  • Serial processing: Focusing on one conversation, then switching to the other. This is what we usually end up doing.

Either way, you're filtering out the unattended conversation. In the filter model of attention, inputs in the margin shift to the focus when certain features of the environment grab your attention.

Remember and Forget

Why you can't remember yesterday's lecture but you remember your first dunk.

Attention lets information in. But what happens next? Your brain has to decide what to keep and what to throw away. That process gives us memory and forgetting, two sides of the same coin.

We'll start with forgetting because, honestly, it's more natural to forget than to remember. And that's actually a GOOD thing.

🤔

Quick: what did you eat for lunch three Tuesdays ago? Probably no idea. But can you still ride a bicycle? Still play a drum pattern you learned years ago? Why can your brain hold onto some things forever and drop others within hours?

Why Forgetting is Normal (and Necessary)

Forgetting is the difference between the amount learned and the amount retained over a period of time without further practice.

But here's the thing: forgetting is actually useful. Imagine remembering everything you were ever taught, from nursery school to now. Every bad experience. Every embarrassing moment. Every random fact. Your brain would be so clogged that you'd hardly function as a rational human being. E be like say our brain get delete button wey dey work on its own. But e no dey delete everything sha.

Theory 1: Trace Decay

The simplest view of forgetting. The trace decay hypothesis says we forget because of the mere passage of time.

When you learn something, a memory trace (or engram) is left in the brain. As time goes on and you don't use what you learned, this engram decays and fades away through normal metabolic processes. Like a path in the bush that grows over if nobody walks on it.

You're more likely to remember things learned recently than things learned long ago. Makes sense, right?

Problems with Trace Decay

Trace decay can't explain everything:

  1. Motor skills persist. You don't usually forget how to dance, cycle, swim, or drive, even after years without doing them. If memories just decayed from non-use, these should be gone too.
  2. Old people remember their youth. Elderly, senile persons often remember events from 50+ years ago vividly, but can't remember what happened yesterday or the names of their grandchildren. If trace decay was the whole story, the oldest memories should be the most decayed. They're not.

So trace decay is part of the story, but not the full picture.

Theory 2: Interference

Interference theory says forgetting happens because materials learned at different times get mixed up and confuse each other when you try to recall them.

There are two types:

Proactive Interference

Earlier learning makes new learning difficult. The old stuff pushes forward in time and disrupts the new.

Example: You learned to drive on the RIGHT in Nigeria. Now in Britain, you keep wanting to drive on the right because the old habit interferes with the new one. That could be dangerous.

Retroactive Interference

New learning disrupts old memories. The new stuff pushes backward and messes with what you already knew.

Example: Learning a new phone number makes you forget your old one. Learning Greek after learning Latin causes confusion about Latin vocabulary.

Retroactive interference works in two ways: (a) confusion/competition between old and new materials, or (b) the new learning actually causes unlearning of the old material.

Now: How Memory Actually Works

How do we remember the things we've learned? Memory involves three related processes:

1

Encoding

Mentally processing information so it can be placed into memory. Converting experiences into a form your brain can store.

2

Storage

Holding that information for a period of time. Keeping it in the system.

3

Retrieval

Accessing or recalling stored memories when needed. Getting the information back out.

Three Types of Memory

Your memory system has three stages, each with different properties:

1. The Sensory Register

Also called the sensory store. Think of it as the reception hall of memory. Everything you see, hear, and feel passes through here first.

Capacity: Huge. It captures everything in your sensory field.

Duration: Extremely brief. Fractions of a second. It's impossible to note all that you see at any given moment. When you look outside a window, you see many things, but you can't take note of all of them. That's the fleeting nature of the sensory store.

Information here that gets your attention moves to short-term memory. Everything else vanishes almost instantly.

2. Short-Term Memory (STM)

The second stage. STM is auditory/echoic/verbal memory. It's active, temporary, and conscious, meaning it holds what you're actively thinking about right now.

Capacity: Small. Only about 7±2 items at a time (that's 5 to 9 chunks of information).

Duration: Very short. Information stays only as long as you keep rehearsing (repeating) it. The moment you stop rehearsing, it starts to fade. Loss in STM is most likely due to trace decay.

In everyday language, this is what we call "cramming." Students summarize notes on small pieces of paper and keep rehearsing all night. As soon as the exam is over, everything disappears. Sound familiar?

To move information from STM to Long-Term Memory, you need elaborate or constructive rehearsal, not just mindless repetition, but actively connecting new information to things you already know.

3. Long-Term Memory (LTM)

The final stage. LTM has a vast, almost unlimited capacity and is relatively permanent. Once information is truly retained here, it stays, although getting it back out (retrieval) can sometimes be difficult.

LTM is highly organized. New items are stored by relating them to materials already there. Information in LTM has meaning. It's arranged more by what it means than by how it looks or sounds.

This is the difference between cramming and actually understanding. Students who truly understand material have it in LTM. They can recall it without further rehearsal because it's connected to meaningful knowledge.

🥁

Drums vs Cramming

Short-term memory is when you cram your notes the night before the exam. You keep repeating the words, over and over, holding them in your head with pure effort. The moment you drop your pen after the exam.. gone. Like it was never there. That's STM.

Long-term memory is like knowing how to play the drums. You haven't played a specific beat in months, but sit behind a kit and your hands still know the pattern. Why? Because that knowledge has MEANING to you. It's connected to real experiences, real emotions, real repetition over years. It entered your LTM through elaborate rehearsal, not cramming. And it stays.

Available vs Accessible

This is a crucial distinction for LTM:

  • Available information: It EXISTS in your LTM. It's stored somewhere in there.
  • Accessible information: You can actually FIND it and retrieve it right now.

All accessible information is available. But available information may not always be accessible. The information is there, you just can't find it at the moment.

📚

Library Analogy

Think of LTM like a university library. A book might be AVAILABLE in the library (it exists, it's been catalogued), but it might not be ACCESSIBLE to you because some unscrupulous student moved it to the wrong shelf, or it's been misplaced. The book isn't gone. You just can't find it right now. That's forgetting in LTM: the information is usually still there, you just can't retrieve it.

Key insight: Forgetting in LTM is most likely due to interference (new or old materials getting in the way of retrieval), not trace decay. The memories don't fade.. they get buried.

What Drives You

The invisible forces behind every single thing you do.

We've covered how the brain works, how we learn, and how we remember. But there's a deeper question underneath all of that: WHY? Why do you eat when you eat? Why do you push yourself to get better at basketball? Why do you care about acing this exam?

The answer is motivation.

🤔

You're reading this right now instead of sleeping. Why? Something is pushing you. Something inside you decided that studying is more important than rest right now. What IS that something?

What is Motivation?

The word comes from the Latin "movere" which means "to move." Motivation refers to the internal processes that energize and direct behaviour toward the achievement of a specific objective.

Motivation works in three stages:

  1. Stimulus (a bodily need, like hunger)
  2. Motive (arousal to action, the drive to do something about it)
  3. Behaviour (the action that brings about goal attainment, like eating)

Category 1: Physiological Motives

Also called primary or maintenance motives. These are unlearned, required for survival, and common to both animals and humans. However, HOW we satisfy them is mediated by learning and culture.

Hunger

Activated by the hypothalamus when glucose (blood sugar) falls below a certain level. What we eat is influenced by learning, emotion, social factors, and economics.

Thirst

Activated when sodium chloride (salt) in the blood becomes too high, causing cell dehydration. Like hunger, learning and culture affect how we satisfy it.

Sleep

So vital that we spend about a third of our lives doing it. Lack of sleep leads to both physiological and psychological malfunctions.

Pain

Leads to avoidance learning. Crucial for survival because it teaches you to avoid things that could harm you. How we react to pain is shaped by learning and experience.

Sex

The most important motive for species survival. Based on hormones: testosterone (males) and oestrogen (females). Controlled by the hypothalamus. In humans, learning heavily influences the sex drive.

Maternal Drive

Stimulated by prolactin hormone which activates milk production. The famous Harlow & Harlow (1966) study found that monkeys raised by their mothers displayed stronger maternal drives than those raised in isolation.

Category 2: Stimulus Motives

These motives are innate but depend more on external stimulation than internal body states.

Activity

The tendency to avoid idleness. Common to all animals. Affected by age, sex, mood, and health.

Exploration & Curiosity

The need to explore, learn, and know. Curiosity is intellectual (wanting to understand), exploration is spatial (wanting to discover new places).

Manipulation

The need to feel and touch specific objects. Often directed at things we find interesting.

Contact

The need to touch and be touched by others. A fundamental social need present from birth.

Category 3: Social Motives

These motives are about our relationships with other people.

Achievement Motive

The need to perform with excellence and measure up to a high, self-imposed standard. This is the drive that makes you want to ace your exam, not just pass it.

Power Motive

The need to gain power and control over others. Shows up in leadership, politics, and competitive environments.

Motivation to Avoid Success

Identified by Matina Horner: some women fear that achieving too much may make them look "masculine" or unattractive. (Note: this research is from a specific historical context and is debated today.)

Affiliative Motive

The need to be with others of a sympathetic group, especially during stress, fear, or anxiety. First-born children tend to have a stronger affiliative motive than those born later.

Other Learned Motives

Fear Motive: The need to avoid or escape the source of fear. Learned in association with pain.

Aggression: Behaviour intended to harm another person physically or psychologically. Freud argued it's innate (from Thanatos). Dollard argued it's caused by frustration (Frustration-Aggression Theory). Men tend to be more aggressive due to testosterone.

Unconscious Motives

According to Freud, the conscious part of human personality is only the tip of the iceberg. Most of what motivates our behaviour is buried in our unconscious. We are not aware of these motives, but we constantly try to satisfy them.

This brings us full circle back to Freud's theory from Chapter 3. The Id's desires, operating beneath the surface, drive more of our behaviour than we realize.

🏀

Why Do You Play Basketball?

Think about it. There's the physiological stuff: the activity motive (your body doesn't want to sit still), the exploration motive (curiosity about what you can do on the court). There's the social stuff: achievement motive (you want to be GREAT, not just okay), affiliative motive (being part of a squad, the bond with your teammates). And if we're being real, maybe some power motive too (that feeling when you dunk on someone and the whole court knows). Multiple motives, all pushing you in the same direction. That's how motivation works. It's rarely just one thing.

Who You Are

What makes you... you. Out of 8 billion people, there's only one Ayo.

We've traveled from the definition of psychology all the way through the brain, learning, memory, and motivation. This is the final chapter, and it's the most personal one. Everything we've learned comes together here: your genetics, your environment, your conditioning, your motives.. they all fuse into one thing. Your personality.

🤔

Think about 4 people you've known for years. Their core personality traits. Have those traits changed? Probably not. That guy who was loud in JSS1 is still loud. That girl who was shy is probably still shy. Personality is stable. But what exactly IS it, and where does it come from?

What is Personality?

Coon (2000) defines personality as a person's unique and relatively stable behaviour patterns. It's the consistency in who you are, have been, and would become.

Personality is the special blend of talents, attitudes, values, hopes, aspirations, and habits that makes you a unique person. Do we all have a personality? Yes. Out of 8 billion people on the planet, no one has the same personality as another.

Traits: The Building Blocks of Personality

A trait is a stable, enduring quality that a person shows in most situations. When you say "Tormusa is intelligent, organized, bold, and friendly," you're describing personality traits.

Traits are remarkably stable. If a friend you've known since childhood as gentle suddenly turns aggressive, they'd become a stranger to you. It would feel like they've lost their personality. But this rarely happens. Personality doesn't change suddenly, barring mental illness or extreme circumstances.

Personality Types: Introvert vs Extrovert

Carl Jung, a Swiss psychiatrist, identified two fundamental personality types:

Introvert

A person who is shy, self-centred, whose attention is focused inward. More interested in their own feelings and ideas than the social environment. When faced with a challenge, they tend to withdraw.

Often good at: science, poetry, solitary work. More interested in non-human subjects.

Extrovert

A person who is bold, outgoing, whose attention is focused outward. Usually talkative, sometimes stubborn and arrogant. Thrives on social interaction.

Often good at: journalism, teaching, social professions. Energized by being around others.

Important: This categorization isn't absolute. A very bold person might show shyness in certain situations, like being bold with peers but reserved around superiors. Most people fall somewhere on a spectrum between pure introversion and pure extroversion.

Self-Concept: Who You THINK You Are

Self-concept is all the ideas and feelings you have about who you think you are. According to Potkay and Allen (1986), it's your perception of your own personality traits.

You build your self-concept from your experiences in life. As you encounter new experiences, you revise and gradually stabilize your sense of who you are. When your lecturer asked you to introduce yourself, what you said? That was your self-concept at work.

Self-Esteem: How You EVALUATE Yourself

Self-esteem has to do with how you evaluate yourself. People with high self-esteem are usually proud, confident, with lots of self-respect. People with low self-esteem feel the opposite.

When you succeed or receive praise, your self-esteem goes up. When you fail, it goes down. Over time, your experiences shape a relatively stable level of self-esteem.

Allport's Classification of Traits

Gordon Allport (1961) identified several kinds of traits. The most important distinction:

  • Common traits: Shared by most members of a particular culture. For example, the Igbo are often described as industrious and showing resilience in business.
  • Individual traits: Unique personal qualities that distinguish YOU from everyone else.

Allport further classified individual traits into three levels:

Most Powerful

Cardinal Traits

A single dominant trait that ALL of a person's activities revolve around. Very rare. Not everyone has one.

Example: The late Chief Gani Fawehinmi's cardinal trait was fairness. Everything he did, every case he took, every cause he championed, traced back to his sense of justice and fair treatment.

Core

Central Traits

The core traits that characterize an individual's personality. Usually 5-10 traits. These are what comes to mind first when someone mentions your name.

Example: "When I think of [friend's name], I think: funny, kind, hardworking, and always late." Those are their central traits.

Situational

Secondary Traits

Less consistent traits that appear only in certain situations. Considered somewhat artificial or occasional.

Example: Your usually serious uncle who cracks jokes only at family parties. That humor is a secondary trait, not a core part of who they are.

The Perseverance of Traits

Once we form a personality trait inference about someone, that inference takes on a life of its own. You may recall thinking your friend is "kind and helpful" long after you've forgotten the specific instance when they helped an elderly person across the street.

Trait inferences are made quickly based on minimal information, and then they persist long after the original evidence has been forgotten. This is a fundamental feature of how humans perceive each other.

🏀

You're More Than Your Height

Ayo, think about this. People see you on the court and immediately think "big man, play center, post up." That's them making a trait inference based on one piece of information: your height. But your actual personality is way more complex. You have guard skills, handles, you can dunk, you draw anime, you play drums for church, you're a Yoruba guy from Abuja studying Criminology in Lafia.

You are LAYERS. Allport would say those people are looking at a surface-level trait (tall = big man) and ignoring all your individual traits. That's the perseverance of traits in action: people form quick impressions and hold onto them even when the evidence says otherwise. Sound familiar?
