03 Culture

What is Culture?

It's way more than music and art

Most people think culture means music, art, and dance. And yes, those are parts of culture. But culture is MUCH bigger.

Culture is "the product of man's past and his environment." It's determined by climate and environment. It's everything a society creates and practices. And it has two major components:

Material culture is the physical things a society makes. Technology, tools, buildings, art, clothing, musical instruments. Things you can touch and hold.

Non-material culture is the ideas, beliefs, customs, values, morals, language, political and social structures, and worldviews. Things that exist in the mind and in how people relate to each other.

Together, culture is described as a "complex mixture of personalities, material objects, patterns of behavior, subtle emotional relationships, accepted intellectual ideas and customary individual actions." It is the dynamic social heritage, transmitted from generation to generation. Continuity and change are its core features.

🍲

Think about cooking. The pot you cook with is material culture.. it's a physical object. But the recipe your mother taught you, the way you season jollof rice, the belief that food should be shared with neighbors.. those are non-material culture. The pot is material. The recipe is non-material.

04 Culture

Five Things That Make Culture.. Culture

And you already know all of them from braiding hair

You've been plaiting hair since you were three years old. That single fact proves ALL FIVE characteristics of culture. Let me show you.

1. Culture is a social creation. Different communities develop their own values and practices based on their needs. Yoruba threading is completely different from Fulani braids. Each community created styles that reflect THEIR identity.

2. Culture is transmissible. It gets passed down from generation to generation. You learned braiding by watching the women around you. Your grandmother taught your mother, your mother taught you, and someday you'll pass those skills to the next generation. That's culture being transmitted.. like a relay baton.

3. Culture is learned. Nobody is born knowing how to cornrow. You practiced, made mistakes, and slowly got better. Language, religious practices, eating habits, dressing styles.. all deliberately learned from parents, schools, and the community around you.

4. Culture is dynamic. It changes and improves over time. Knotless braids, butterfly locs, passion twists.. these styles didn't exist twenty years ago. They evolved from older techniques, influenced by creativity and social media. Culture never sits still.

5. Culture is societally shared and enforceable. There are aspects of culture that every member of the community is expected to respect. At certain traditional ceremonies, specific hairstyles are expected.. you can't just show up however you want. There are mechanisms and sometimes even social consequences to keep things consistent.

The answer: Over 350!

Nigeria has over 350 distinct ethnic groups, each with their own customs, traditions, and languages. Three hundred and fifty. Let that sit for a second.

Archaeological evidence shows that parts of Nigeria have been settled since the Paleolithic era (that's the Old Stone Age, roughly 500,000 to 9,000 BC). People have been living, building, and creating culture in this land for hundreds of thousands of years.

The largest and most politically dominant groups are the Yoruba, Hausa, Igbo, and Fulani. Other prominent groups include the Edo, Ibibio, Tiv, Nupe, and Kanuri.

🌍

Geographic Context

Southern Nigeria (forest belt): Dense forest environment. Home to the Yoruba and Edo in the southwest, and the Igbo and Ibibio in the southeast.

Northern Nigeria (grassland-savanna): Open grasslands. Home to the Hausa, Fulani, and Kanuri in the far north.

The Middle Belt (central Nigeria): The area between north and south, containing over 250 small ethnic groups, including the Tiv and Nupe. This area has the highest concentration of small, distinct peoples anywhere in Nigeria.

The Niger and Benue rivers flow through the country and meet at a confluence point, roughly marking the boundary between north and south.

Over the last three and a half centuries, southern peoples experienced more European influence (trade, Christianity, Western education), while northern peoples had greater contact with trans-Saharan influences (Islamic trade routes, Arabic learning). This geographic positioning shaped everything: political systems, religion, trade, and education.

Since Christian missionaries brought Western-style education to the south first, the southern forest peoples have historically produced the largest number of educated and highly trained people in Nigeria's workforce.

🎧

Think of Nigeria like a massive playlist with 350 different artists. Some artists (Yoruba, Hausa, Igbo, Fulani) have the most plays. Others are smaller but just as unique. And depending on where they're based (forest or savanna), their sound is completely different.

A significant and tragic feature: the Middle Belt, with its many small groups and limited fighting forces, was historically vulnerable to raids from larger, more powerful groups. The result was that the Middle Belt became a major source region for enslaved people, traded both across the Sahara to the north and across the Atlantic to the west.

Key Takeaways

  • Nigeria has 350+ ethnic groups, settled since the Paleolithic era
  • Two major regions: forest (south) and savanna (north) with Middle Belt between
  • Geography shaped which external influences (European vs Islamic) each region experienced

06 Nigeria's Peoples

The Yoruba People

City builders, master craftsmen, and proud democrats

What if I told you there's a Nigerian ethnic group that was building big, organized cities while many other societies were still figuring things out? Meet the Yoruba.

Yoruba Profile

The Yoruba are concentrated in southwestern Nigeria: Ogun, Osun, Oyo, Ondo, Ekiti, Kwara, Lagos, and part of Kogi states. They share a common language and trace descent from a common ancestor called Oduduwa, who is believed to have established the Ife dynasty.

The Yoruba had a unique dual leadership system:

The Oni of Ife was the spiritual ruler of all Yoruba people. Think of Ile-Ife as the sacred heart of Yoruba civilization.

The Alafin of Oyo was the political leader. He ran the day-to-day governance from the powerful Oyo kingdom.

It's like having both a spiritual guide and a team captain.. each with their own authority.

Unlike most Nigerian peoples, the Yoruba had a unique and long-standing tradition of living in large towns. Major Yoruba towns include Ibadan, Ogbomosho, Oshogbo, Ilorin, Abeokuta, Ilesha, and Ede.

The extended family is the basic social unit. Each extended family has a territorial existence (the large traditional compound) within the town, plus a distinct area of farmland in the surrounding rural areas.

Division of labor: Men are primarily farmers, women are primarily traders. There's also a significant number of professional artists and craftsmen.

The Yoruba excel in wood carving and other crafts. The famous terracotta and bronze heads of Ife are about 800 years old and testify to incredibly high-quality craftsmanship.

Think about it.. you can look at a really well-done cornrow and just know the person who did it is a master. That's what the Ife bronze heads are. Eight hundred years old, and the detail still amazes experts today.

The Yoruba are known as a deeply religious people with great respect for superiors and elderly people.

Yet they are also extremely independent, diplomatic, and resentful of despotic leadership. They don't follow leaders blindly. This quality showed up clearly during the pre-colonial Yoruba civil wars AND in modern Yoruba politics since Nigerian independence.

Respect AND independence.. the Yoruba balance both.

Key Takeaways

  • Yoruba trace descent from Oduduwa at Ile-Ife
  • Dual leadership: Oni of Ife (spiritual) + Alafin of Oyo (political)
  • Unique urban tradition with major towns like Ibadan and Abeokuta
  • Master craftsmen.. the 800-year-old Ife bronze heads are legendary

The Yoruba built kingdoms and cities. But their eastern neighbors.. the Igbo and Ibibio.. organized their societies in a completely different way. Same forest, different system.

07 Nigeria's Peoples

The Igbo and Ibibio

Village life, family compounds, and a different kind of order

Same forest environment. Same types of crops. Completely different political systems. Why? The answer might surprise you.

Like the Yoruba, the Igbo and Ibibio are settled agricultural people. But unlike the Yoruba, they live in small villages, not towns. Urbanization in eastern Nigeria is a phenomenon of the colonial period, with only a few exceptions like the Onitsha Igbo and the Efik of Calabar.

One of the most striking features of Igbo and Ibibio culture is how family compounds are spread out across the village territory, rather than being clustered together in a center. In areas like Awka, Owerri, Nsukka, Abak, and Ikot Ekpene, you'd see homes and compounds dotted across the landscape. Compact, village-style settlements survived mainly in the sparsely settled forest areas of Ikom, Calabar, Bende, and the Niger Delta.

Their political systems were "stateless" or decentralized. There was no single central ruler like the Yoruba had. Instead, leadership was distributed among community members based on personal qualities and influence.

🏀

Imagine a basketball game with no coach, no captain, no referee. Everyone just plays based on mutual respect. Whoever is clearly the most skilled naturally gets the ball more. That's kind of how stateless societies worked.. no central ruler, but leadership still happened through personal qualities and community agreement.

An important observation: the Yoruba and Edo peoples of the west had greater contact with the empire-building groups of the far north than the eastern peoples did. This may have influenced the development of different political styles. The forest peoples' traditional religion also remained almost completely insulated from Islam until the colonial period, when Christianity was introduced.

Key Takeaways

  • Igbo and Ibibio lived in villages, not cities
  • Family compounds spread across the land, not clustered together
  • No single central ruler.. leadership based on personal qualities
  • Same environment as Yoruba but completely different political system

Why did some groups build kingdoms while others didn't? The Middle Belt peoples help us answer that question.

08 Nigeria's Peoples

The Middle Belt Peoples

Why some societies had kings and others didn't

The Tiv people had no king, no chief, no council of elders with real power. When the British tried to find "someone in charge" to work with, they literally couldn't find anyone. Here's why that's actually brilliant, not backward.

The Middle Belt of Nigeria has over 250 small ethnic groups, including hill-dwelling peoples of the Jos Plateau and the eastern highlands of Adamawa. The two largest are the Tiv of the Benue valley and the Nupe of the middle Niger valley.

The Tiv are probably the most extreme of Nigeria's "stateless societies." Their political structure was highly fragmented. The only unit someone could claim responsibility for was the compound, which was essentially a domestic (household) unit, not a political one.

This created a serious problem for the British policy of "Indirect Rule" (the colonial system of governing through existing local leaders). Among the Yoruba, you could work through the Alafin. Among the Hausa, you could work through the Emir. But among the Tiv? There was nobody with executive powers of any kind to work through!

But don't mistake "no central ruler" for "no leaders." The Tiv DID have leadership. Men of great affluence and impeccable personal qualities were readily recognized as leaders. It was just that their authority came from respect, not from a title or position.

🔑

The Key Insight

Here's the pattern: the so-called "stateless" societies of Nigeria consist of groups whose ethnic territory is so crowded that people experience acute shortage of farmland.

In Tiv land, Igbo land, and Ibibio land, the heavy demand for farmland made it impossible for any one person to control all the land centrally. Everyone needed their own piece.

Where land ownership is dispersed, political authority is also dispersed.

In Yoruba and Benin areas, farmland was abundant. One authority could control it and distribute it. That's how centralized kingdoms formed.

💡

Picture two streets in Gombe. On one street, one big Oga owns all the shops and rents them out.. he makes the rules, everyone follows. On the next street, every person owns their own small shop. Nobody can boss everyone around because everyone has their own thing. That second street is how stateless societies worked.. when land is spread among many people, power spreads too.

Where everybody get their own land, nobody fit come dey boss everybody.

09 Nigeria's Peoples

The Hausa People

Traders, builders, and West Africa's biggest language

You grew up in Gombe. You've heard Hausa spoken your entire life. You probably speak it yourself. But did you know that Hausa is the most widely spoken language in ALL of northern Nigeria and is generally understood in most parts of the northern states? Let's learn more about the people behind the language you already know.

The Hausa peoples are concentrated in Sokoto, Kano, Jigawa, Katsina, Kebbi, Kaduna, Zamfara, and Bauchi states. Politically, they organized themselves as independent city-states (like Kano, Katsina, Zaria, and Sokoto) which were never unified until the Fulani conquest in the early 19th century.

Islam was introduced to Hausa land as far back as the 14th century, but it only became widely adopted after the Fulani jihad led by Uthman dan Fodio. Today, most Hausa are Muslim, and their political, social, land tenure, and legal systems are deeply influenced by Islamic principles.

Hausa economy is built on intensive agriculture: guinea corn, millet, maize, and beans. They are one of the few grassland peoples who practice traditional irrigation. River flood plains called Fadama (rich, wet soil left behind after floods) are put under intensive cultivation during the dry season. Instead of waiting for rain, they farm the flood plains. Smart, right?

The Hausa are also skilled craftsmen and probably the most travelled traders in West Africa. Their craft products include leather goods, wood carvings, and perfumes. They're largely responsible for organizing the massive cattle-kolanut trade between western and northern Nigeria.

10 Nigeria's Peoples

The Fulani and the Kanuri

Nomads, conquerors, and an empire that refused to fall

The Fulani are probably the only ethnic group in Africa with no distinct territory. They're found in almost every country of the Sudan zone, from Senegal in the west to the upper Nile in the east. They migrated into Nigeria from the Senegal valley and settled among the Hausa. Their arrival is part of a general migration that started between the 12th and 13th century and spread them across the entire West African savanna.

Then came the game-changer: the Fulani jihad led by Uthman dan Fodio in the early 19th century, which conquered the Hausa city-states and transformed the political landscape.

But here's where it gets interesting. There are TWO completely different types of Fulani.

The Kanuri: The Empire That Stood Firm

The Kanuri of Borno and Yobe states are also Muslims, but with a key difference: Islam was already well established in Borno BEFORE the Fulani jihad. The Kanuri didn't need the Fulani to bring them Islam.

Politically, the Kanuri built the Kanem-Borno Empire, one of the longest-lasting empires in African history. It survived for centuries and incorporated many communities and hundreds of thousands of people.

And here's the important part: the Fulani tried multiple times to conquer Borno, and they NEVER succeeded. The Kanuri held their ground. That's remarkable given how successfully the Fulani conquered the Hausa city-states.

Like the Hausa, most Kanuri are settled cultivators and traders. Many own large herds of cattle managed by Shuwa Arabs or the Cattle Fulani.

Key Takeaways

  • Fulani have no distinct territory.. spread across all of West Africa
  • Cattle Fulani: nomadic herders. Town Fulani: settled administrators who spread Islam
  • Kanuri built the Kanem-Borno Empire, had Islam BEFORE the jihad
  • Kanuri successfully RESISTED Fulani conquest

11 Origin Stories

How Peoples Explain Their Origins

Three categories of Nigerian origin traditions

Every family has an origin story. "Your great-grandfather came from this village and married a woman from that town." Nigerian ethnic groups have the same thing, but on a massive scale. And their stories fall into exactly three categories.

Category 1: External Migration. These traditions tell of a culture-hero who migrated from somewhere OUTSIDE present-day Nigeria, usually from Arabia or northeast Africa. The hero leads a group, arrives in a new area, and establishes a dynasty. This type is very common. Examples: Bayajida (Hausa), Oduduwa (Yoruba), Sayf b. Dhi Yazan (Kanuri).

Category 2: Indigenous / Autochthonous. These traditions don't remember a time when the area was ever empty. The people were always there. There's no migration story, no foreign hero. Examples: the Igbo (no external origin tradition), the Ibibio (trace ancestry only to the Benue valley), the Ijaw (movements within the Niger Delta only). The Igala claim connections with Yoruba, Edo, Nupe, and Igbo, with migrations around the Niger-Benue confluence, and a founding prince from Benin who established Idah.

Category 3: Creation Stories. These traditions explain the ULTIMATE origins of a people by reference to creation itself. They say that the first human was created at a specific location. Examples: the Nri/Eri tradition among the Igbo (first man descended from the sky at the Niger-Benue confluence), the Yoruba (Ile-Ife is where the world was created), the Edo (first man created at Benin).

The Fulani stand somewhat apart: their migration from Senegambia in the 12th-13th century is a well-established historical fact, not myth.

Some have always been in the same neighborhood, as far as anyone remembers (Category 2). And some have this epic origin scene where they literally came into existence on screen (Category 3).

The Legend of Bayajida

A prince, a sword, a snake, and seven kingdoms

This story hits like a K-drama plot. A prince runs away from home after family drama, crosses an entire desert, marries a princess in a foreign land, gets too popular and has to flee AGAIN, picks up a special sword, kills a monster to save a town, and then the queen falls for him. Follow Bayajida's Journey

Click each stop in order to follow the legend from Baghdad to Daura.

Baghdad: Bayajida was a prince of Baghdad in Arabia. After a serious quarrel with his father, he fled his homeland. His journey would take him across the entire Sahara Desert.

Kanem-Borno: He arrived among the Kanuri people and married a Kanuri princess. He gradually built a large following. But the Mai (king) grew suspicious of his growing influence. To avoid the anger of the Mai, Bayajida fled westward with his followers.

From this union, seven children emerged who became the founders of the seven principal Hausa states: Kano, Daura, Katsina, Zaria, Gobir, Rano, and Biram.

According to tradition, Bayajida also had seven children by a concubine given to him by the queen. These children were said to be the founders of Zamfara, Kebbi, Gwari, Yoruba, Jukun, Nupe, and Yauri (the neighboring states).

🌟 Journey Complete! Bayajida's legend connects Baghdad to the founding of the Hausa states. Versions vary between communities, but the outline stays the same.

See hero wey show! Na so the man take save the whole town.

Other Migration Traditions

Bayajida isn't the only migration legend. The Kanuri tell of Sayf b. Dhi Yazan, a culture-hero who migrated from Arabia across the Sahara and successfully unified pre-existing settlements (the Sao, Zagawa, and others) on the eastern shore of Lake Chad. This led to the rise of the Kanuri dynasty and eventually the Kanem-Borno Empire.

The Yoruba tradition says that Oduduwa was a prince who migrated from Arabia (some versions say northeast Africa) after the establishment of Islam. He crossed the Sudan and Hausa land, eventually settling at Ile-Ife, which became the dispersal center for the Yoruba people.

Meanwhile, the Igbo had no tradition of external origin until recently. The Ibibio have two traditions, both emphasizing that their ancestry cannot be traced beyond the Benue valley. The Ijaw only reference movements within the Niger Delta. The Igala claim connections with Yoruba, Edo, Nupe, and Igbo, and believe their founder was a prince from Benin who established Idah.

💡

Important Note

Notice something about the Hausa tradition? It says that Bayajida's children by a concubine founded "Yoruba" among other states. But the Yoruba have their OWN origin story with Oduduwa. This is normal.. different groups tell the story differently, and each places themselves at the center. These traditions explain how people understand their IDENTITY and connections, not literal historical sequence.

Key Takeaways

  • Bayajida: Baghdad prince who founded the seven Hausa states at Daura
  • Sayf b. Dhi Yazan: Arabian hero who unified Kanuri communities at Lake Chad
  • Oduduwa: migrated to Ile-Ife, which became the Yoruba dispersal center
  • All migration legends mention PRE-EXISTING communities already living there

In the Beginning

Creation stories and what all these traditions have in common

The Yoruba didn't just say "Ile-Ife is where our people started." They said Ile-Ife is where the WORLD was created. And the story of how it happened is unforgettable.

The Nri/Eri Tradition (Igbo)

Among the Igbo, the Nri tradition indicates that the heartland from where the Igbo dispersed was the area of the Niger-Benue confluence. The first man, "Father Nri" (also called Eri), descended from the sky at this sacred location.

The Yoruba Creation Story

Before the creation of Ile-Ife, the legends say, the whole world was covered with water. God then sent out a party of men to create land out of the water. They were given three things: pieces of iron, a handful of soil, and a five-toed cockerel.

Obatala was supposed to lead the group. But he got drunk from too much palm wine. Oduduwa seized the authority, led the party, and descended at the present site of Ile-Ife by means of a chain.

Here they performed the creation: the iron was placed on the water, the soil placed on the iron, and the five-toed cockerel made to spread the soil. The water receded, and so the earth was created.

It was from Ile-Ife, the first land created, that all Yoruba land and indeed the whole world was peopled!

The Edo people also believe that the first man was created at Benin.

🙏

This story might remind you of Genesis. In the beginning, everything was water. God sent a group down to create land. The chosen leader stumbled (palm wine instead of the serpent's temptation), and someone else had to step up. The tools were different.. iron, soil, and a special cockerel.. but the pattern of divine creation from nothing is something your faith already knows deeply.

What Do All These Traditions Tell Us?

Looking across ALL the origin traditions of Nigerian peoples, three important patterns emerge:

1. They concern the emergence of dynastic states, not the original peopling of the land. These legends are about how organized political systems and ethnic identities FORMED, not about how humans first arrived.

2. Most traditions refer to relatively recent times, especially after the rise of Islam. They're not ancient prehistoric accounts.

3. Virtually ALL traditions mention pre-existing communities in the areas that became the center of each ethnic group. Bayajida found people already at Daura. Sayf found the Sao and Zagawa at Lake Chad. Even the Yoruba creation narrative involves outsiders arriving to transform what already existed.

Glossary

Alafin of Oyo

The political leader of the Yoruba people, based at Oyo

Autochthonous

Indigenous.. people who have always been in an area, with no tradition of migration from elsewhere

Bororo

Another name for the Cattle Fulani, the nomadic herding branch of the Fulani people

City-state

An independent political unit consisting of a city and its surrounding territory

Culture

The dynamic social heritage of a people, including everything they create, believe, practice, and pass down

Culture-hero

A legendary figure credited with founding or transforming a people's identity and territory

Dynasty

A sequence of rulers from the same family line

Ethnic group

A community of people who share common customs, traditions, language, and identity

Fadama

River flood plains used for farming during the dry season, a traditional Hausa irrigation practice

Fulani gida

The Town or settled Fulani, who live in conquered Hausa towns as administrators

History

The sum total of human activities in the past, and the study of change over time

Indirect Rule

The British colonial system of governing through existing local leaders and traditional authorities

Jihad

A religious campaign.. in this context, the Fulani holy war led by Uthman dan Fodio in the early 1800s

Kanem-Borno Empire

The long-lasting empire built by the Kanuri people near Lake Chad

Mai

The title for the ruler of the Kanuri/Borno kingdom

Material culture

The physical, touchable objects a society creates.. tools, art, buildings, technology

Non-material culture

The ideas, beliefs, customs, values, language, and social structures of a society

Oni of Ife

The spiritual ruler of all Yoruba people, based at Ile-Ife

Paleolithic era

The Old Stone Age period, roughly 500,000 to 9,000 BC, the earliest period of human tool use

Stateless society

A society with no centralized political authority.. leadership is distributed, not concentrated in one ruler
